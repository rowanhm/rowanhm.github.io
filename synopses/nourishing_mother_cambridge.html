Scattered across Cambridge are reliefs depicting an old university emblem, from the year 1600. It shows a woman, naked, lactating, beneath which there is an inscription: Alma Mater Cantabrigia. This is where the Americanism ‘alma mater’ comes from. Translated into English, the inscription reads: Nourishing Mother Cambridge.

In his feature-length debut, director Rowan Hall Maudslay presents an ethnographic portrayal of Cambridge, a town in the east of England which is famous around the world because it houses a prestigious university with a very long history. On one level, ‘Nourishing Mother Cambridge’ is about all the peculiar idiosyncrasies that give texture to this town: it is a film about monasteries, a river, latin mottos, tourists, and eels. But on a different level, ‘Nourishing Mother Cambridge’ is a film about time: a film about all the subtle ways that the past reverberates in the world around us, everywhere we look.
